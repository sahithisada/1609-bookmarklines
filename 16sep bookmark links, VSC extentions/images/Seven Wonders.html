7 WONDERS

The New 7 Wonders of the World was a campaign started in 2001 to choose Wonders of the World from a selection of 200 existing monuments. The popularity poll via free web-based voting and telephone voting was led by Canadian-Swiss Bernard Weber and organized by the New 7 Wonders Foundation (N7W) based in Zurich, Switzerland, with winners announced on 7 July 2007 at Estádio da Luz in Lisbon. The poll was considered unscientific partly because it was possible for people to cast multiple votes.According to John Zogby, founder and current President/CEO of the Utica, New York–based polling organization Zogby International, New 7 Wonders Foundation drove "the largest poll on record". The program drew a wide range of official reactions. Some countries touted their finalist and tried to get more votes cast for it, while others downplayed or criticized the contest. After supporting the New 7 Wonders Foundation at the beginning of the campaign by providing advice on nominee selection, the United Nations Educational, Scientific, and Cultural Organization (UNESCO), bound by its bylaws to record and give equal status to all World Heritage Sites, distanced itself from the undertaking in 2001 and again in 2007. The 7 winners were chosen from 21 candidates, which had been whittled down from 77 choices by a panel in 2006. The New 7 Wonders Foundation, established in 2001, relied on private donations and the sale of broadcast rights and received no public funding. After the final announcement, New 7 Wonders said it did not earn anything from the exercise and barely recovered its investment. Although N7W describes itself as a not-for-profit organization, the company behind it—the New Open World Corporation (NOWC)—is a commercial business. All licensing and sponsorship money is paid to NOWC. The foundation ran two subsequent programs: New 7 Wonders of Nature, the subject of voting until 2011, and New7Wonders Cities, which ended in 2014

CHINA

THE GREAT WALL OF CHINA

The Great Wall of China is an ancient series of walls and fortifications, totaling more than 13,000 miles in length. Built over centuries by different Chinese dynasties, the wall was primarily constructed to protect the Chinese empire from invasions, especially from nomadic groups from the north.

Its construction began as early as the 7th century BC, but it was most famously expanded and reinforced during the Ming Dynasty (1368–1644). The wall is made of various materials, including stone, brick, tamped earth, and wood, depending on the region and time period of construction. The Great Wall is not a continuous line but rather a collection of walls that branch off and reconnect. It traverses diverse terrains, from mountains to deserts, and is a symbol of China’s enduring strength and ingenuity. While it no longer serves a military purpose, it remains one of the most iconic architectural feats in human history and a UNESCO World Heritage Site.

JORDAN

PETRA JORDAN

This ancient city, hidden within rugged desert cliffs, was once the thriving capital of a wealthy kingdom. Known for its rock-cut architecture, the city's most famous structures are carved directly into the reddish-pink sandstone, earning it the nickname Rose City.

It flourished as a major trading hub, connecting distant regions through commerce, particularly for incense, spices, and other goods. Over time, it was abandoned and largely forgotten by the outside world, until its rediscovery in the early 19th century. Today, it is renowned for its monumental tombs, temples, and intricate facades, with its main entrance passing through a narrow gorge, revealing a stunning and intricate stone treasury. The site remains one of the world's most celebrated archaeological treasures, symbolizing the architectural brilliance and ingenuity of its creators.

PERU

MACHU PICCHU

Machu Picchu is an ancient Incan city located high in the Andes Mountains of Peru. Built in the 15th century, it is believed to have been an estate for the Inca emperor Pachacuti or a religious site. The city's impressive architecture, featuring terraced slopes, temples, and intricate stone construction, showcases the Inca's advanced engineering skills. Machu Picchu is known for its "dry-stone" construction, where stones were precisely cut to fit together without mortar, making the structures incredibly resilient. The site is set dramatically on a mountain ridge, surrounded by lush green peaks and often shrouded in mist, which adds to its mysterious beauty.

Abandoned during the Spanish conquest, Machu Picchu remained hidden from the world until it was rediscovered in 1911 by American explorer Hiram Bingham. Today, it is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and one of the most famous archaeological sites globally, drawing millions of visitors for its stunning views and historical significance.

ITALY

ROMAN COLOSSEUM

The Roman Colosseum, also known as the Flavian Amphitheatre, is one of the most iconic structures of ancient Rome. Built between 70-80 AD under the emperors Vespasian and Titus, it was the largest amphitheater ever constructed and could hold up to 50,000-80,000 spectators. The Colosseum was primarily used for gladiatorial contests, public spectacles, animal hunts, and executions.

Its design, featuring a complex system of vaults and arches, allowed for efficient crowd management and seating arrangements. Beneath the arena floor, there was a network of tunnels and chambers, where animals and gladiators were kept before being brought into the arena. Although partially ruined due to earthquakes and stone robbers over the centuries, the Colosseum remains a symbol of Roman engineering brilliance and the grandeur of the Roman Empire. Today, it is one of the most visited tourist attractions in the world and a UNESCO World Heritage Site, serving as a reminder of the empire's power and the cultural importance of public entertainment in ancient Rome.

MEXICO

CHICHEN ITZA

Chichen Itza is an ancient Mayan city located in the Yucatán Peninsula of Mexico. It was one of the largest and most powerful cities in the Maya civilization, flourishing between the 9th and 12th centuries. The city is renowned for its well-preserved monuments and sophisticated architectural designs, reflecting both astronomical and religious significance. At the heart of Chichen Itza stands El Castillo, or the Temple of Kukulcán, a massive step pyramid. This pyramid is aligned with the equinoxes, when the sun creates a shadow that resembles a serpent slithering down the steps, symbolizing the feathered serpent god, Kukulcán.

Other significant structures include the Great Ball Court, where a ritual game was played, and the Temple of the Warriors.with its intricate carvings and columns. The city's layout reflects the Maya's deep understanding of mathematics, astronomy, and engineering, as well as their religious practices. Today, Chichen Itza is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and one of the most visited archaeological sites in Mexico, celebrated for its cultural and historical importance.

BRAZIL

CHRIST THE REDEEMER

The Christ the Redeemer statue, located in Rio de Janeiro, Brazil, is one of the most recognizable landmarks in the world. Standing atop the Corcovado Mountain at an elevation of 2,310 feet (710 meters), the statue overlooks the city and the bay. Completed in 1931, Christ the Redeemer is a towering figure of Jesus Christ with outstretched arms, symbolizing peace and welcoming. The statue is 98 feet (30 meters) tall, with an arm span of 92 feet (28 meters).

It is made of reinforced concrete and covered with thousands of triangular soapstone tiles, which help it withstand the weather. The statue was designed by Brazilian engineer Heitor da Silva Costa and French sculptor Paul Landowski. It has become a global symbol of Christianity and a prominent cultural icon of Brazil. In 2007, Christ the Redeemer was named one of the New Seven Wonders of the World due to its impressive scale and symbolic significance.

INDIA

TAJ MAHAL

The Taj Mahal is a stunning white marble mausoleum located in Agra, India. Commissioned by the Mughal Emperor Shah Jahan in 1632, it was built in memory of his beloved wife, Mumtaz Mahal, who passed away during childbirth. The construction was completed in 1653, employing a workforce of thousands of artisans, architects, and craftsmen. The Taj Mahal is renowned for its architectural beauty and is a masterpiece of Mughal architecture, combining elements from Islamic, Persian, Ottoman Turkish, and Indian architectural styles.

The central dome rises to about 240 feet (73 meters) and is surrounded by four minarets. The structure is adorned with intricate marble inlays and carvings, and the surrounding gardens and reflecting pools enhance its grandeur. The mausoleum is set within a large complex that includes a mosque, a guest house, and beautifully landscaped gardens. The Taj Mahal is not only a symbol of eternal love but also a UNESCO World Heritage Site and one of the most visited and admired monuments globally.
